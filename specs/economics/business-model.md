## 6. Unit Economics Model (Core)

```python
# Core unit-economics assumptions (per subscriber per week)
price_per_week           = 50    # revenue per subscriber per week (USD)
items_per_box           = 2     # number of garments (e.g. top+bottom) sent weekly
cost_per_item           = 50    # average wholesale cost per garment (USD)
uses_per_item           = 20    # assumed rental cycles per item before retire
cleaning_cost_per_item  = 2     # cost to clean each garment (USD)
shipping_cost_per_box   = 34    # round-trip shipping & handling per box (USD)

# Derived weekly costs
depreciation_per_item_per_week = cost_per_item / uses_per_item
weekly_inventory_cost         = depreciation_per_item_per_week * items_per_box
weekly_cleaning_cost          = cleaning_cost_per_item  * items_per_box

profit_per_user_per_week = price_per_week - (weekly_inventory_cost + weekly_cleaning_cost + shipping_cost_per_box)
```

*   **Contribution margin:** ~14% gross margin is extremely tight and must cover all overhead, implying that scale and cost control are crucial. We expect heavy sensitivity to shipping and usage: if shipping or cleaning are higher or if items last <20 uses, margins evaporate (see below).
    
*   **Sensitivity analysis:** Key levers include shipping rates, rental lifespan, and number of items. For example, if shipping can be reduced (flat-rate USPS or negotiated UPS) or if we increase to 3 items/box, the per-user economics change markedly. We summarize ranges:

Monitor and fix any operational glitches (e.g. box labeling, web signup flow). **Phase 2 (Days 30–60):** Collect intensive feedback from first users: conduct interviews on fit, style, and satisfaction. Optimize user questionnaire and style rules based on data. Begin light digital ads and partnerships (e.g. local gyms or coworking spaces could distribute promo). Onboard 100 subscribers. Tweak pricing or offering if user feedback suggests misalignment. **Phase 3 (Days 60–90):** Ramp up marketing (social, influencer, email referrals) targeting 200–300 users. Introduce a referral program (e.g. “Give 10%, get 10% off”). Streamline the tech for styling (e.g. refine the algorithm based on real feedback). Prepare a press campaign with sustainability angle. Throughout 90 days, track KPIs daily (see below). This phased, feedback-driven roll-out ensures we learn quickly and adapt before full-scale spend.

## 13. KPI Framework

We will monitor metrics weekly and monthly. **Revenue/Financial:** subscribers (active and net adds), Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR), Average Revenue per User (ARPU ≈$200/mo at base), and gross margin per subscriber. We'll calculate LTV and Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) to ensure a healthy LTV:CAC ratio (RTR’s ARPU ~$197 vs CAC $150–$200  [gadallon.substack.com](https://gadallon.substack.com/p/rent-the-runway-when-complexity-collides#:~:text=Similarly%2C%20subscriber%20economics%20reveal%20the,saturation%20or%20unsustainable%20customer%20economics)   suggests LTV/CAC struggles). **Churn & Retention:** % subscribers retained month-over-month; target >80% retention (20% or lower monthly churn). **Engagement:** average number of wears/outfit (if tracked via app feedback). **Operational:** Utilization (# rentals per item), garment loss/damage rate, average laundry turnaround time, and shipping cost per box (benchmarked vs carrier rates  [routific.com](https://www.routific.com/blog/economy-shipping#:~:text=,60)  ). **Customer:** fit success rate (e.g. % “fits well”), Net Promoter Score (NPS), and customer satisfaction ratings. **Marketing:** channel conversion rates (ad click-to-sub, influencer ROI), and cost per click/acquisition. All KPIs will have targets (e.g. gross margin >20%, CAC < 3 months’ revenue), and we will review them weekly to guide tactics.

## 14. Experiment Plan (6–10 Experiments)

1.  **Pricing Sensitivity Test:** Offer half of new sign-ups a $50/week price and half $60/week to gauge lift and conversion difference.
    
2.  **Box Content Variant:** Test 1-outfit/week vs. 2-outfits/2-weeks (larger box fortnightly) to see if customers prefer frequency or volume.
    
3.  **Size Fit Quiz Version:** A/B test a detailed sizing questionnaire vs. a simpler one, measuring return rates and fit satisfaction.
    
4.  **Marketing Message:** Run parallel campaigns highlighting (A) sustainability vs. (B) convenience/styling, to see which drives higher engagement.
    
5.  **Referral Incentive:** Offer “Give/Get $25” referral credits and measure increase in sign-ups vs. period without it.
    
6.  **Onboarding Promotion:** Test free first week vs. straight $50 signup, to see impact on trial-to-paid conversion.
    
7.  **Partner Offer:** Trial a cross-promotion with a plus-size fashion blog or fitness studio (e.g. first 50 customers get extra day), measuring sign-ups.
    
8.  **Outfit Preference:** Give some subscribers an optional “X item preference” to see if allowing one free swap improves retention.
    
9.  **Packaging/Returns:** Compare customer satisfaction/returns if we use USPS flat-rate box instead of UPS to measure cost and UX difference.
    
10.  **Sustainability Impact Messaging:** Measure customer sign-up rate in markets when we explicitly mention “5 garments saved from landfill” vs. no sustainability claim.
    

Each experiment will run for 2–4 weeks with clear hypotheses (e.g. “Higher price reduces sign-ups by X% but increases revenue”) and success metrics.

## 15. Recommendation

Based on our analysis, **$50/week as a base price is likely too low to be profitable without very high scale or cost concessions**. The unit economics (≈14% gross margin) and fixed costs suggest break-even requires well above 250 subscribers (likely 500+). **Recommendation:** proceed with a pilot **but do not commit full resources to $50/week as final pricing** until tested. The MVP should start smaller (e.g. initial offering $50–60/week for one outfit, with a streamlined catalogue) to validate key assumptions. We should be prepared to adjust to a higher price (e.g. $60-$70/week) or a biweekly delivery model if unit economics demand it. Key first steps:

1.  **Pilot launch with flexible pricing** – test demand at $50/week vs $60/week (and possibly a two-tier plan) to determine willingness-to-pay.
    
2.  **Build operations for a small cohort** – finalize fulfillment and laundry partnerships and acquire ~50 test customers to refine supply chain.
    
3.  **Develop technology and measurement** – implement the fit quiz and tracking to start collecting the data needed to improve personalization and measure unit costs (e.g. track actual shipping/cleaning spend).
    

If pilot results show that unit economics cannot be improved (e.g. if we can’t cut shipping or increase uses), we should reconsider the $50/week target. If initial customers love the service and retention is strong, we can iterate pricing or offer structure accordingly. In summary: **Proceed with a lean MVP (go), but treat the $50 price as an assumption to verify, not a fixed mandate.** Monitor KPIs closely, and prioritize the 3 actions above in the first quarter.
